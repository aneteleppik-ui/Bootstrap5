Inside the Book...

A quick glance at the topics you'll learn

The Asian small-clawed otter is the smallest otter species in the world but one of the most intelligent and social. Found in Southeast Asia, these otters live in family groups where they cooperate to hunt, communicate, and raise their young.

They use their front paws with remarkable skill to open shells, grab crabs, and handle small objects. Highly playful and vocal, they are known for their constant chatter and curious personalities, which make them a favorite species in zoos and wildlife documentaries.

The marine otter, native to the rocky coastlines of Chile and Peru, is one of the few otters that spends most of its life in the ocean. It feeds mainly on fish, sea urchins, and crustaceans, skillfully navigating through waves and underwater caves.

Unlike many freshwater otters, this species relies on dense coastal vegetation and rocky shelters for protection. Unfortunately, habitat destruction and pollution have made the marine otter one of the most endangered otter species in South America.

The North American river otter is a playful and adaptable mammal that thrives in lakes, rivers, and wetlands across the continent. Its sleek body, webbed feet, and thick waterproof fur make it an excellent swimmer even in freezing conditions.

River otters are known for sliding down snowy riverbanks, wrestling, and chasing each other for fun. Once heavily hunted for their pelts, they have made a strong comeback thanks to conservation programs that protect clean waterways and natural habitats.

The smooth-coated otter is a large, muscular species found throughout South and Southeast Asia. It gets its name from its short, glossy fur that gives it a sleek, polished look. These otters live in close-knit groups and are highly vocal, using whistles, chirps, and growls to communicate.

They often hunt cooperatively, catching fish with impressive teamwork. However, their population is declining due to river pollution, habitat loss, and human disturbance in the regions where they live.

The neotropical otter inhabits rivers, streams, and wetlands across Central and South America. It is a shy and elusive species, spending much of its time hidden among dense vegetation. Its diet consists mainly of fish, but it also eats amphibians and small mammals.

Despite living in warm tropical climates, it faces serious threats from deforestation, water contamination, and illegal hunting. As a top predator in its environment, the neotropical otter plays an important role in maintaining healthy freshwater ecosystems.

Endangerment

How endangered are they?

Asian small-clawed otter

Their populations are rapidly declining because of habitat loss, water pollution, and the illegal pet trade. They’re hunted and captured across Southeast Asia, and many wetlands where they live are being destroyed for farming and urban development. Conservation programs exist, but their future remains uncertain.

Reviewed by Anete
Marine otter

The marine otter is one of the rarest and most threatened otter species. It depends heavily on clean, rocky coastlines, but human activity, oil spills, and fishing nets have made survival difficult. Its small, isolated populations make it highly vulnerable to extinction.

Reviewed by Anete
North American river otter

This species is currently safe and thriving in many parts of North America. After near extinction due to overhunting, successful conservation and clean water laws helped them recover strongly. They are now common in many regions again.

Reviewed by Anete
Smooth-coated Otter

While still relatively widespread in South and Southeast Asia, smooth-coated otters are facing declines due to pollution, habitat destruction, and conflict with fishermen. They are also targeted for their pelts. In some areas, their numbers are stable, but in others, they are disappearing quickly.

Reviewed by Anete
Neotropical otter

The neotropical otter's biggest threats are deforestation and water contamination in Central and South America. Although it still has a wide range, its population is becoming fragmented, and sightings are increasingly rare. Protecting rainforest rivers is crucial for its survival.

Reviewed by Anete
